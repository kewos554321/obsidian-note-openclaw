#### 📚 Today's Concept: Revenue vs. Net Income

What it is: Revenue is the total money a company earns from selling its products or services before any costs are deducted. Net income is what remains after subtracting all expenses, including cost of goods sold, operating costs, interest, and taxes.

Why it matters: A company can have high revenue but still lose money if expenses are out of control. For software engineers, think of revenue as raw user sign-ups and net income as actual profit after server costs, salaries, and marketing—the true measure of business health.

Example: A SaaS company reports $10 million in annual revenue. However, it spent $6 million on cloud infrastructure, $3 million on salaries, and $2 million on sales—total expenses of $11 million. Net income = $10M - $11M = -$1 million loss. High revenue, but unprofitable.

Rule of thumb: If revenue grows but net income shrinks or stays flat, watch for rising costs or pricing pressure—it’s a warning sign of inefficiency, not success.

#### 🧩 Blind 100 — 153. Find Minimum in Rotated Sorted Array [Binary Search]
**連結:** https://leetcode.com/problems/find-minimum-in-rotated-sorted-array/
> 📅 **Today's Daily Challenge:** #3852 Path Existence Queries in a Graph II [Hard] — Tags: Array, Two Pointers, Binary Search, Dynamic Programming, Greedy, Bit Manipulation, Graph Theory, Sorting — https://leetcode.com/problems/path-existence-queries-in-a-graph-ii/

**Problem Type:** Binary Search on Rotated Array  
**Key Insight:** The minimum element is the only element where the previous element is greater than it (pivot). In a rotated sorted array, the right half is always smaller than the left half when a rotation exists.  
**Approach:**  
1. Set `l, r = 0, len(nums)-1`.  
2. While `l < r`:  
   - Compute `mid = (l + r) // 2`.  
   - If `nums[mid] > nums[r]`: minimum is in the right half → `l = mid + 1`.  
   - Else: minimum is in the left half (including mid) → `r = mid`.  
3. Return `nums[l]`.  

**Python3 Solution:**  
```python
class Solution:
    def findMin(self, nums: List[int]) -> int:
        l, r = 0, len(nums) - 1
        while l < r:
            mid = (l + r) // 2
            if nums[mid] > nums[r]:
                l = mid + 1
            else:
                r = mid
        return nums[l]
```

**Complexity:** Time O(log n) | Space O(1)  

**Blind 100 Note:** Classic binary search on rotated array — teaches how to handle "unknown pivot" by comparing with the right boundary. Similar problems: *Search in Rotated Sorted Array*, *Find Peak Element*.  

**Contest Tips:**  
- Edge case: array not rotated → loop still works (returns first element).  
- Use `nums[mid] > nums[r]` not `nums[mid] > nums[l]` — the right side is the safe anchor for rotation detection.  
- Python trick: `//` for integer division, no overflow issues.  
- Common mistake: using `<=` in condition or forgetting `mid+1` when moving left — test with `[3,4,5,1,2]`.

#### 📷 Today's Concept: Special — Film Photography Aesthetic and Grain Simulation

**What it is:** A digital workflow that mimics the look of analog film—soft halation, muted color, and organic grain—rather than sterile digital sharpness. It’s about embracing imperfection to evoke nostalgia or mood.

**Why it matters:** Film aesthetic adds emotional weight and timelessness to portraits and street scenes, while grain masks digital noise and gives cinematic video a textured, “lived-in” feel.

**How to apply it:**
1. **Underexpose slightly** (–0.3 to –0.7 EV) to preserve highlights and mimic film’s shoulder roll-off.
2. **Use a diffusion filter** (e.g., Tiffen Black Pro-Mist 1/4) to soften sharpness and bloom highlights.
3. **In post, add grain** (Lightroom: Amount 20–30, Size 25, Roughness 50) or use Dehancer/VSCO film presets.
4. **Desaturate and shift hues**—pull greens toward yellow, blues toward cyan, and lift shadows into muted teal or brown.
5. **For video**, shoot in S-Log2 or S-Cinetone, then apply a film LUT and add grain in DaVinci Resolve (Fusion’s “Film Grain” node).

**Sony A7C tip:** Set **Picture Profile to PP7 (S-Log2)** for maximum dynamic range, then grade with a film LUT. This preserves highlight roll-off better than standard profiles.

**Common mistake:** Adding grain *before* exposure and color grading. Grain becomes harsh and unnatural. Always grade first, then add grain as the final layer—it sits on top of the image, not baked into it.

#### 📚 Today's Concept: CAP Theorem

**What it is:** The CAP Theorem states that a distributed data system can only guarantee two of three properties simultaneously: Consistency (all nodes see the same data), Availability (every request gets a response), and Partition Tolerance (system works despite network failures). In practice, you must choose between CP (sacrifice availability) or AP (sacrifice consistency) because network partitions are inevitable.

**When to use it:** Use when designing distributed databases or microservices that must handle network failures. For example, a banking system prioritizes CP (consistency over availability) to prevent double-spending, while a social media feed prefers AP (availability over consistency) to keep showing posts even if some are slightly stale.

**Example:**  
Choosing AP with Cassandra (tunable consistency):  
```java
// Write with eventual consistency
Statement write = QueryBuilder.insertInto("users")
    .value("id", 1).value("name", "Alice")
    .setConsistencyLevel(ConsistencyLevel.ONE); // AP: fast, but stale reads possible
```

**Gotcha:** CAP is not a “pick two” choice at all times—it applies only during a partition. When the network is healthy, you can have all three. Misunderstanding this leads to over-engineering for rare partition scenarios.
